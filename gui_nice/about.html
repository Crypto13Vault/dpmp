Dual-Pool Mining Proxy (DPMP)

DPMP is a lightweight, high-reliability Stratum v1 mining proxy designed to sit between one or more miners and two upstream mining pools. It was designed primarily for Bitcoin solo-miners who run one or more ASIC miners (SHA-256) and want to split hash power across compatible coins/pools (i.e., BTC/BCH/DGB). Some miners, like the NerdQAxe++, have a built-in "dual pool mining" feature but the majority of miners out there do not.

So if you have one or more miners that do NOT have a built-in dual-pool mining feature (such as the Canaan Avalon Q or the Nano3S) and you would like, for example, to mine to a BTC pool and a Bitcoin Cash pool simultaneously, then DPMP is for you.

DPMP enables deterministic dual-pool scheduling, advanced difficulty/extranonce handling, and observability via Prometheus metrics — while remaining simple to deploy and operate.

At a high level, DPMP:

  • Accepts Stratum connections from miners (acts like a pool)
  • Maintains concurrent Stratum connections to two upstream pools (acts like a miner)
  • Routes jobs and share submissions according to a scheduler with manual or auto-balancing
  • Ensures correct propagation of difficulty, extranonce, and job IDs
  • Exposes runtime metrics for monitoring and analysis
  • Logs structured, machine-readable events for debugging and auditing

The proxy is intentionally transparent: miners and pools do not need to be modified or aware that DPMP is in the middle.

Please visit the DPMP landing page at: https://ckryza.github.io/dpmpv2/.

Choosing Your Pools

Before configuring DPMP: Connect each miner directly to each pool you plan to use and confirm you are receiving accepted shares within a reasonable time. If a miner cannot produce accepted shares when connected directly, DPMP cannot fix that — it's a miner/pool compatibility issue.

Fleet-Aware Scheduling

DPMP's scheduler uses a fleet-aware assignment strategy that evaluates each miner's hashrate, health score, and firmware capabilities to determine the optimal role for every miner in the fleet. Rather than switching all miners simultaneously between pools — which causes dramatic ratio oscillations, reject storms, and unnecessary wear on sensitive devices — the scheduler divides the fleet into Static and Dynamic categories.

Small miners are assigned statically to whichever pool needs their hashrate to meet the target weight ratio, where they mine continuously without ever switching. Only the minimum number of miners necessary are designated as Dynamic time-slicers, and these are chosen based on hashrate fit and health score to ensure the most capable and reliable miners handle the switching workload.

This approach eliminates the "herding behavior" of the original implementation, where the entire fleet would stampede from one pool to the other and back again, producing wild swings in the actual ratio and floods of rejected shares. Instead, the target ratio is maintained smoothly by one or two Dynamic miners alternating between pools on calculated time intervals, while the rest of the fleet mines undisturbed.

The result is faster convergence to the target weights, dramatically lower fleet-wide reject rates, and graceful handling of mixed fleets containing miners with varying capabilities and firmware quirks.

Pool Compatibility and Miner Protection

DPMP presents itself as a ckpool-compatible Stratum v1 proxy to all connected miners, regardless of what upstream pool types are configured. This means your miners see a single, consistent mining endpoint — they never need to know that Pool A might be running MiningCore while Pool B runs Bassin (ckpool). Behind the scenes, DPMP handles the translation between different pool behaviors, including differences in difficulty management (VarDiff), extranonce formatting, job delivery timing, and share validation. Each miner maintains its own independent connection to both upstream pools through DPMP, and the scheduler seamlessly routes work and shares to the correct pool based on the current allocation weights.

From the miner's perspective, it is simply connected to one stable pool that occasionally sends new work — exactly as a direct ckpool-type connection would behave.

Automatic Miner Compatibility Detection

Some miners cannot handle mid-session changes to their extranonce parameters, which is required when switching between pools that use different extranonce sizes (for example, a 4-byte pool and an 8-byte pool).

When DPMP detects this incompatibility, it adapts automatically. On the first pool switch attempt, if a miner accumulates 10 or more consecutive rejected shares with zero accepts, DPMP flags that miner's IP as extranonce-sensitive and immediately reconnects it back to its original pool.

On all subsequent switch attempts, rather than trying to change the extranonce mid-session (which the miner would ignore, producing invalid shares), DPMP gracefully closes the miner's connection and sets a handshake hint pointing to the target pool. The miner reconnects within about one second, performs a fresh handshake directly with the new pool, and begins mining immediately with the correct parameters — no rejected shares, no manual intervention. A 30-second cooldown between forced reconnects provides an additional safety net against excessive disconnects. This entire process is transparent to the miner and requires no configuration; DPMP learns each miner's capabilities and adjusts its switching strategy accordingly.

Dynamic Miner Reject Rates and Pool Switching

Miners assigned to the Dynamic (time-slice) category will typically show a higher reject rate in their individual dashboards compared to statically-assigned miners. This is a normal and expected side effect of how DPMP operates. When a Dynamic miner receives a pool switch command, it must flush its internal work pipeline — any shares it has already computed but not yet submitted were built using the previous pool's job data, difficulty, and extranonce parameters. These in-flight shares are essentially orphaned the moment the switch occurs, because the new pool has no knowledge of the old pool's work templates. The new pool will reject these stale shares as invalid, even though the miner performed legitimate work.

From the miner's perspective, it did everything correctly; the rejects are simply a consequence of the pool context changing underneath it. DPMP mitigates this by suppressing many of these transient rejects internally — intercepting them before they reach the miner and returning fake "accepted" responses — but a small number may still pass through during the brief transition window.

Importantly, these rejects do not represent lost hashrate or wasted electricity in any meaningful sense; the miner immediately begins productive work on the new pool within one to two seconds of the switch.

Hashrate Allocation

DPMP supports three modes for allocating hashrate between the two pools: Slider, Oracle Auto-Balance, and Manual Assignment. You can switch between modes at any time from the Home tab using the arrow buttons at the top of each panel — no restart required.

Slider

When you select the Slider panel on the Home tab, you can adjust the hashrate allocation (weighting) in real time with no restart required. Just drag the slider to the desired position and DPMP will adjust accordingly. Some limitations do apply. The slider ranges from 5% to 95%, so you cannot allocate 100% of the hashrate to one pool or the other. If you DO want either 0/100 or 100/0 weightings, you must set that in the Scheduler Settings section on the Config tab and restart DPMP. And if your weighting is set to either 0/100 or 100/0, the Slider panel will no longer be available on the Home tab. Bottom line: if you need to temporarily move some hashrate from one pool to the other, the Slider panel is the best way to do that.

Oracle Auto-Balance

If you are mining to a BTC pool and a BCH pool you will be able to switch to the Oracle Auto-Balance panel on the Home tab. When in Oracle Auto-Balance mode, DPMP will automatically adjust the pool weighting ratio periodically based on the observed real-time network hashrate of the coin associated with each pool. The default pool ratio in Oracle Auto-Balance mode is 50/50 and it adjusts from there. The goal of this feature is to send more hashrate to the pool where our hashrate has more "value". So, for example, if BTC short-term network hashrate increases relative to its long-term hashrate while BCH hashrate decreases, then DPMP will automatically adjust the weighting to send more hashrate to BCH and less to BTC.

The hashrate information is obtained by polling an oracle API endpoint at regular intervals (default is every 10 minutes) and the oracle data is used to calculate the optimal pool weighting ratio based on the relative hashrate changes. The oracle URL, poll interval, and max deviation threshold can all be configured in the Scheduler Settings section on the Config tab. The Oracle Auto-Balance panel will show the current oracle-based pool weighting ratio as well as the real-time network hashrate for each pool.

Our "baseline" network hashrate calculation tracks closely with what you'd see on hashrate reporting sites (e.g., CoinWarz), while the short-term window deliberately captures block-to-block variance to detect transient hashrate shifts — the Oracle Auto-Balancer acts on the ratio between the two, not the raw numbers.

Limitations

Currently, Oracle Auto-Balance only supports BTC and BCH pools. In the Pool A Settings and Pool B Settings sections of your configuration you must specify a chain name for each pool (e.g., BTC or BCH). If you are using a combination of pools that is not BTC/BCH then you won't be able to use Oracle Auto-Balance, but you can always use the Slider panel or the Manual Assignment panel instead. We also limit the max deviation threshold to between 5 and 45. With a setting of 5, the Oracle Auto-Balancer will never move beyond a 45/55–55/45 ratio. With a setting of 45, it will never move beyond a 5/95–95/5 ratio. The default max deviation value is 20, so the Oracle Auto-Balancer will never move beyond 30/70–70/30.

Chart Updates

The network hashrate charts only update when the data points (short-term and long-term) actually change. So even though the default polling interval is every 10 minutes, the charts may not update on every polling cycle if the network hashrate values have not changed.

Manual Assignment

When you switch to the Manual Assignment panel on the Home tab, you can then go to the Stats tab and manually assign each miner to either Pool A or Pool B by using the dropdown in the Pool column of the Fleet Stats table. Once you make an assignment for a miner, that miner will be pinned to the assigned pool and will not be scheduled to switch pools until you change the assignment. This is useful if you want to manually balance the hashrate across pools by miner instead of by hashrate percentage.

If you switch back to either the Slider panel or the Oracle Auto-Balance panel, then normal operation for those panels will resume.

Note on Switching

When you switch a miner from pool A to B, or Pool B to A, the miner will be disconnected and reconnected to the new pool, which can take a few seconds. During this transition period, the worker name in the Fleet table will turn orange. Once the pool switch is complete the worker name color will return to normal.

Stats Tab

The Stats tab provides real-time visibility into every aspect of your mining fleet. It is organized into three collapsible sections, each containing a sortable table that updates every five seconds. Click any column header to sort the table by that column, and click again to reverse the sort order.

Fleet Stats

The Fleet Stats table gives you a scheduler-level view of your entire fleet. Each connected miner appears as a row showing its current pool assignment (A or B), how long it has been mining on that pool, and whether the scheduler has assigned it as Static (locked to one pool) or Dynamic (actively time-slicing between both pools to achieve the target ratio). The Pinned column indicates whether a miner has been automatically pinned to a single pool due to hardware incompatibility with mid-session pool switching. The Switches column tracks how many times each miner has changed pools since it connected, while Contribution shows each miner's share of total accepted difficulty across the fleet as a percentage. The Health column displays a reliability score from 0.00 to 1.00 that reflects how cleanly each miner handles pool switches over time — a score of 1.00 indicates no issues, while lower scores mean the miner has experienced rejects or disconnects after switches. A totals row at the bottom summarizes fleet-wide switch counts and confirms that contributions sum to 100%.

Manual Assignment Mode

When you are in Manual Assignment mode the Pinned column is not displayed. Instead, use the dropdown in the Pool column to assign each miner to Pool A or Pool B.

Worker Stats

The Worker Stats table focuses on individual miner performance. Each row shows a miner's estimated hashrate over three rolling windows (5-minute, 60-minute, and 24-hour), its current share submission rate, working difficulty, total accepted shares, best share found, reject count, and reject percentage. The Seen column shows how recently each miner submitted a share, and workers that have not been seen for five minutes are automatically removed from the table. At the far right of each row is an on/off toggle button that allows you to temporarily disable a miner without disconnecting it. When toggled off, the miner remains connected to DPMP but is excluded from scheduling and its shares are silently swallowed (the miner receives fake "accepted" responses so it continues hashing without errors). Toggling a miner back on immediately restores it to normal operation. This is useful for testing, maintenance, or temporarily removing a problematic miner from the fleet without physically accessing the device. The toggle state resets to "on" when DPMP restarts.

Pool Stats

The Pool Stats table provides a per-pool summary of connection health and share accounting. Each row represents one of the two configured pools and shows its slot (A or B), detected coin type, the extranonce2 size advertised by the pool, the current ratio of hashrate allocated to that pool, the pool's estimated 5-minute hashrate, and the upstream connection latency. The accepted and rejected share counts reflect cumulative totals since DPMP started, along with the corresponding reject percentage. The Jobs column shows the total number of mining notifications received from each pool, and TotalDiff shows the sum of all accepted share difficulties. Note that accepted and rejected counts may include data from workers that have since disconnected.

Pool Failover Protection

DPMP has pool failover protection implemented as follows: if you are splitting work between pools A and B (i.e., pool weighting ratio is NOT 0/100 or 100/0) then if either pool fails, all work (100%) will be routed to the pool that is still active. When the inactive pool becomes available again, the original weighting logic will resume. If BOTH pools become unavailable, DPMP will simply wait until one or both pools become available again and, during this time, it will maintain a connection with any miners attached. If you are currently routing to only one pool (i.e., pool weighting is 0/100 or 100/0) then if the active pool becomes unavailable, DPMP will wait until the pool becomes available again — in this situation it will NOT route any work to the pool with the 0 weighting.

Pool Idle Timeout Handling

Some upstream pool software (such as MiningCore) implements an idle timeout that automatically disconnects any worker session that has not submitted a share within a set period (typically around 10 minutes). This is not a failure — it is the pool quietly closing a connection it considers inactive. For DPMP, this can affect the upstream connection to whichever pool a miner is not currently mining to, since that pool never receives any share submissions from that miner.

DPMP handles pool idle timeouts gracefully. When an upstream pool closes an idle connection, DPMP silently absorbs the disconnect and does not reconnect immediately — there is no reason to maintain a live connection to a pool that a miner is not currently using. The miner continues mining on its active pool completely undisturbed, with no health score penalty and no time-on-pool reset. If the scheduler later assigns that miner to the pool that had gone idle, DPMP reconnects on demand, bootstraps the session, and completes the switch — typically within 15–30 seconds. During this transition the miner remains connected and mining on its current pool until the new connection is fully ready. Note that after an on-demand reconnect, the new pool connection starts fresh with its minimum configured difficulty and ramps up over the first minute or two — this is normal pool VarDiff behavior and not an error.

Configuration

Idle timeout handling is configured independently for each pool using the Disconnect Idle Connections toggle in the Pool A Settings and Pool B Settings sections of the Config tab. Enable it only for pools that implement idle timeouts (such as MiningCore). Leave it off for pools that maintain persistent connections regardless of share activity (such as Bassin / ckpool).

Configuration and Setup

When DPMP first starts, go to the Config tab and edit the Pool A Settings and Pool B Settings sections. For both pools, enter the URL/IP Address, Port, and the wallet address to be used when mining to that pool (make sure the wallet address is correct!). You can also give each pool a display name and assign it to a particular blockchain.

By default, work will be split 50/50 between the two pools. You can adjust the weighting in the Scheduler Settings section (e.g., 70/30, or 90/10 — whatever you like), or you can leave it at 50/50 and use the Slider panel on the Home tab to adjust the weighting in real time. If you want to use the Oracle Auto-Balance feature, make sure you have set up a BTC and BCH pool as described in the Oracle Auto-Balance section above.

In the Pool A Settings and Pool B Settings sections you will also find two additional options worth reviewing:

  • Disconnect Idle Connections — Enable this if your pool software (such as MiningCore) automatically closes upstream connections that have not submitted a share within a set period. When enabled, DPMP will not attempt to keep an idle upstream connection alive for miners that are not currently assigned to that pool. Leave this off for pools that maintain persistent connections regardless of share activity (such as Bassin / ckpool). See the Pool Idle Timeout Handling section below for more detail.
  • Force Reconnect on EN2 Size Mismatch — When enabled, if a miner's extranonce2 size does not match what the pool advertises during the handshake, DPMP will force a reconnect to re-negotiate the correct parameters rather than attempting to continue with a mismatched session. This is recommended for pools that are strict about extranonce2 sizes. See the Automatic Miner Compatibility Detection section above for background on how extranonce handling works.

The Scheduler Settings section also includes an SR Recruitment Exclusions field. This allows you to specify a comma-separated list of worker names that should never be dynamically recruited as additional time-slicers by the SR (Share Ratio) feedback system. Excluded miners can still participate in the fleet as static miners — they simply will not be promoted to Dynamic (time-slicing) duty by the automatic SR correction mechanism. This is useful for miners that are known to behave poorly during pool switches (such as those with firmware that crashes on extranonce renegotiation) or miners that you want to keep stable on a fixed pool assignment at all times.

Once you make the configuration changes, click the APPLY + RESTART button and DPMP will restart. When it does, check the Home tab and make sure you see DPMP: running, and the tables on the Stats tab should begin to populate.

Now, for each miner you want to use with the proxy, set up the miner's Pool URL to point to the IP address of the box on which you are running the DPMP proxy, set the Port to 3351, and put in one of your wallet addresses, a period, and finally the worker name (i.e., <wallet address>.<workername>).

Pool Address Book

DPMP includes a built-in Pool Address Book that makes it easy to manage and switch between multiple mining pools without re-entering connection details every time. Pool entries are saved automatically — whenever you click APPLY on the Config tab, both Pool A and Pool B are added to the address book if they aren't already stored there. Each entry records the pool's host, port, name, wallet address, and blockchain. To use a saved pool, open either the Pool A Settings or Pool B Settings expansion on the Config tab and click Select from Address Book. A dialog will appear listing all saved pools — click Select next to any entry to instantly fill in all fields for that pool slot. You can manage your saved pools at any time using the Edit Address Book button, which allows you to rename entries for easier identification or delete pools you no longer use. Note that the address book only prevents duplicate entries from being created automatically — if you update a pool's wallet or port, you can use the Edit dialog to keep your entries current.

Important — Wallet Address

The wallet address you input in the miner is NOT used by the proxy — only the worker name is used. The proxy gets the correct wallet address for each pool from the DPMP configuration file. So if you are setting up DPMP to mine to a Bitcoin pool and a Bitcoin Cash pool, in your miner config use either your BTC or BCH wallet address (does not matter which) and then append the worker name as shown above.

Once your miners are configured and restarted, check the Home tab and you should see the correct number of miners connected, and the Accepted, Rejected, and Jobs stats should be updating. You should also see your miner(s) show up on your pool dashboards as well as in the Worker Stats table on the Stats tab.

Note on Reject Rates

When using DPMP, it is normal for the rejection rate reported in your miner dashboard to be slightly higher than usual. Switching between pools introduces some job not found and stale job conditions. This is expected behavior and nothing to be concerned about.

How Many Miners Can DPMP Support

Running DPMP on an AMD Ryzen 5 3550H with 16 GB RAM under umbrelOS 1.5, this setup should easily support up to 50 miners with a hashrate range from 1 to 200 TH/s. Note that higher hashrate miners actually produce fewer shares per second (pool VarDiff scales up). A 1 PH/s miner at diff 65536 submits roughly 1 share every 4 seconds. So a farm of 100 x 300 TH/s miners might only generate ~50 shares/sec total, which is very manageable. The ceiling isn't raw CPU — it's the single-threaded event loop getting saturated by JSON processing and share routing.

Farm Size Miners Total Hashrate Hardware
Small 2 – 30 Up to 5 PH/s Raspberry Pi 5 (8 GB) or any mini-PC
Medium 30 – 75 5 – 50 PH/s 4-core (Ryzen 5 / Intel i5), 16 GB RAM
Large 75 – 200 50 – 200 PH/s 8-core (Ryzen 7 / Intel i7), 32 GB RAM
XL 200 – 500 200+ PH/s 12+ core, 64 GB RAM, or split across 2 DPMP instances

Things to keep in mind:

  • Upstream pool connection limits — Some pools cap connections per IP/wallet. Each miner in DPMP creates a separate upstream connection per pool, so 100 miners = 200 upstream connections (100 to Pool A, 100 to Pool B). Public pools may throttle this; self-hosted pools typically do not.
  • The real scaling path is multiple DPMP instances, each handling a subset of miners. Since DPMP is lightweight, you could run 3–4 instances on the same hardware, each on a different port, splitting the fleet. This also adds redundancy.
  • Log volume scales linearly with miners. At 200 miners, the JSON logging can become significant disk I/O, so suppress all high-frequency events in production via the Logging Settings section on the Config tab.

Error Reporting

If you encounter errors associated with a particular miner or pool:

  • Make sure that ALL events are being logged — check the Logging Settings section on the Config tab
  • Click APPLY + RESTART to restart DPMP and recreate the error
  • Click the Download Log button on the Logs tab
  • Send a description of the problem (i.e., miner-related or pool-related and identify the miner or pool) to dpmp.project@gmail.com and attach the log file.

We have tested DPMP with a number of different miners and pools but it is by no means an exhaustive list. The log data you provide will help us to expand the list of supported miners and pools.
